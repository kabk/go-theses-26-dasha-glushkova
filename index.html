Schooling

intro

Hmmm

part 1

I like structures. Visible or invisible, I find joy in shaping thoughts and ideas into a format I can work with, even when they’re still chaotic in my head. This drew me to higher education, even in something as abstract as art. This essay is my attempt to make sense of the four years at the Royal Academy of Art, The Hague.

Before I begin, I need to say something about how I’m writing this text. In his essay On Lightness, Italo Calvino argues that literature has the power to lift the weight of living, to make heavy things bearable through language that doesn’t crush the reader under its own density. He’s interested in how writing can address serious subjects without becoming ponderous, how it can maintain precision without sacrificing accessibility. I’ve spent years resenting academic writing. Not because theory lacks value, but because so much of it deliberately excludes, wrapped in language that keeps people out rather than inviting them in.

Paul Kingsnorth addresses this directly in The Language of the Master, where he shows how institutional language is designed to mystify rather than clarify. The master’s language maintains distance between those who know and those who are kept from knowing. It creates hierarchies through opacity. Kingsnorth argues that this isn’t accidental or simply poor writing. It’s a deliberate strategy to preserve power, to make knowledge seem more complex than it is so that only certain people can access it. When institutions speak in ways that exclude, they’re protecting their authority.

This essay is my practice of what Calvino calls lightness. I’m taking ideas about education and power and making them accessible without making them shallow. If I’m going to critique institutions for their opacity, I can’t hide my critique behind impenetrable prose.

Growing up, I was a “gifted student.” Every class came easily. I found excitement in homework. At 14 I transferred to another school because my parents felt I wasn’t challenged enough. The first academic struggles came soon after. Among hundreds of other “gifted students” I turned out to be ordinary. I knew how to solve tasks in the way they were meant to be solved, but that wasn’t what teachers wanted anymore. They wanted to train our critical thinking and ability to adapt, but only in ways that would turn us into “creative” thinkers within their specific understanding. I failed at that. The more time passed, the less I engaged with the systemic approaches we were taught. So I stopped trying.

By the time I applied to kabk, I had built an entire world in my head about what art school would be. I’d read about EU art education, about schools where students and teachers worked together, where creativity wasn’t something to be trained into a specific shape but something to be discovered. The architecture itself seemed to promise something: glass-walled studios where you could see everyone working, where transparency wasn’t just a design choice but a philosophical commitment. I wanted teachers who would treat me like a person, not a student number. Underneath it all was a single desire that tied everything together: permission to think differently, to make mistakes, to become something other than what the system had already decided I should be.

At 19 I was accepted to kabk. At 20 I began my studies.

kabk’s graphic design department has a reputation built over decades. People talk about it as one of the good art schools, the kind that produces actual designers who go on to do actual work. That reputation shaped everything. When we got an assignment, I was convinced it was meaningful not just because of what it asked us to do, but because it came from this place, this institution. These assignments felt like pathways to becoming a real “graphic designer,” whatever that meant. The myth of the school’s prestige made everything feel significant.

Everything felt serious. The stress was almost unimaginable, especially during assessment periods. This wasn’t casual exploration or playful experimentation. It was high-stakes, intense. At 10pm, the only students remaining at the academy were the graphic design ones. Every assignment felt like it mattered enormously. We believed it did partly because the institution’s reputation told us it did, and partly because for many first-year students this school was the only anchor in a new country.

I was still figuring out how to exist in this new space, trying to understand what graphic design even was for me. Everything still emerging and uncertain. I had arrived at the place I’d imagined, and I was ready to become whatever it would help me become.

Part 2

In my second year, our typography teachers Johanna Ehde and Elisabeth Rafstedt were fired.

The reason was simple: the institution employed two typography teachers on two full salaries teaching one class. Budget cuts came. They decided to lay off both and hire one part-time position instead. It was economics, nothing more.

What followed was a discourse about who deserved to be fired. Students were invited to participate, to voice opinions, to argue for why these particular teachers should stay. We wrote an open letter. We collected signatures. We organized meetings. The institution thanked us for our input. The decision had already been made.

Johanna and Elisabeth were well-liked. The classroom was peaceful, quiet. They asked us what we were interested in, what materials or techniques we wanted to test. Most importantly, they didn’t inflict their vision of our projects upon us. In her book Teaching to Transgress, bell hooks distinguishes between two models of education. The first is what Paulo Freire calls the banking model, where teachers deposit knowledge into passive students as if they were empty vessels. The second is education as the practice of freedom, where learning happens through dialogue, mutual recognition, and genuine encounter between teacher and student. Johanna and Elisabeth were actually doing this. They treated us as whole people with our own questions and interests, not as raw material to be shaped into their vision of what a designer should be.

Students rallied. It didn’t matter. The teachers were fired anyway.

What became visible was the gap between what the institution claimed to value and what it actually protected. All the rhetoric about student-centered learning and community meant nothing against budget calculations. Our solidarity, our collective voice, our carefully articulated arguments were structurally powerless against decisions that had already been made on economic logic, not merit or pedagogy.

We learned that institutional decisions aren’t made by community input, even when institutions perform the theater of consultation. We learned that being right doesn’t matter. We learned that awareness without power is just suffering.

This firing revealed the structural bind teachers exist in: if a teacher commits fully to teaching, prioritizes pedagogy and student development, they become professionally invisible. Students and institutions alike begin to see them as someone who “couldn’t make it” as a designer, someone who teaches because they can’t do. But if a teacher maintains their own practice, keeps their design work primary and treats teaching as secondary income, students sense it. They sense when a teacher doesn’t have the heart for teaching. They sense it through one-on-one communication, through the teacher’s commitment to the class, through feedback that feels perfunctory rather than engaged, through moments when the teacher pushes their vision onto student work instead of figuring out solutions together.

Students are right to sense this. It’s not paranoia or entitlement. It’s accurate perception of someone who’s treating teaching as exploited labor, as the thing they do to afford their actual practice.

The institution benefits from this arrangement. It gets teaching labor cheaply while maintaining prestige through teachers who are “active practitioners.” It doesn’t have to choose between good teaching and impressive credentials because it can extract both, simultaneously, from people who are trapped trying to do two jobs at once.

There is no correct answer within this system. A teacher who commits to teaching becomes professionally invisible. A teacher who maintains their practice can’t fully commit to teaching. The institution profits from this impossibility while students and teachers both suffer its consequences.

I started helping my peers with typography around this time. Partly because I was decent at it and people kept asking, but also because I wanted to. I wasn’t qualified. But the absence of teacher-student power dynamics allowed for conversation, where the “dumb” questions became just questions. What excites me is this: because I’m upfront about the fact that my theoretical base is much more sparse than any teacher’s, it created a conversation where my temporary student didn’t take my words as seriously as they would from a teacher. It’s a shaky position, mostly because I’m not sure how to keep the same dynamic as my knowledge grows. Is it possible to not be in a power position if you are the one sharing knowledge?

This was when I first considered teaching. To explain something to someone else, I had to simplify it first, break down what I thought I knew into its actual components. And then I had to find a way to present it so they could discover it themselves rather than just receive it from me. I was learning as much as they were, maybe more. I was learning what I actually understood versus what I’d just memorized. I was learning how to articulate things. They were learning typography, but I was learning how to teach. It was one of the most rewarding things I’ve done within design.

I had stopped waiting for the institution to be what it claimed to be.

Part 3

By third year, my friend Sasha and I started running peer-to-peer workshops called Playdate.

This initiative came from pure desire and lots of speculative sleepovers. We wanted to learn more or everything, to gather people, to create spaces where students from different departments could share skills and knowledge they felt passionate about. We tried to get institutional funding. We were met with promises and “allies” – the same people we’d hoped would fund us. The irony wasn’t lost on us. We rearranged our personal budgets to accommodate biweekly meetings.

Every couple of weeks the workshops brought together a diverse group of people from across the academy. We did practice and theory-based workshops: Stitch & Bitch where our friend Puck talked about the feminist history of knitting and then guided an introductory workshop, or Getting Unstuck, where my classmate and colleague, Jasper created exercises for people to relieve themselves of their fear of a blank paper.

In their book The Undercommons, Fred Moten and Stefano Harney describe spaces of learning that exist outside institutional approval, spaces where people study what they actually need to know rather than what institutions tell them they should know. They call these fugitive spaces the undercommons. Playdate was this. We created the learning spaces we wanted to create.

The institution couldn’t support what we were doing. Not wouldn’t, just couldn’t. The structure of institutional education can’t accommodate learning that emerges from collective desire rather than curriculum planning, that crosses departmental boundaries, that doesn’t produce credits or assessable outcomes. When Kenneth Goldsmith argues in Infallible Processes that sometimes the process of learning itself teaches more than any content could, he’s describing something institutions claim to value but structurally cannot support.

We built something meaningful. It worked. Students wanted it, participated in it, learned from it. And the institution’s inability to support it, despite all its rhetoric about interdisciplinary learning and student initiative, revealed more clearly what institutions actually value: control, measurability, outcomes that fit within existing structures.

Playdate didn’t solve the problem of institutional education. It made the problem more visible.

When I sat with someone and helped them work through a typography problem, I was discovering what it meant to teach. I learned to articulate design principles in ways that made sense to me and then to someone else. I learned to listen for the moment when a question would shift their thinking. I learned to step back and let them find their own breakthrough rather than imposing mine. I was learning through doing, discovering what teaching meant through the practice of it.

Teaching stopped being an abstract possibility. Not a career backup plan, but an integral part of my practice, something I was already doing and wanted to do more intentionally.

I had stopped looking to the institution for validation or support. I was creating alternatives, building structures outside the official ones. But I was also watching my idealized vision of higher education decay completely, recognizing how the institution was fundamentally unable or unwilling to provide what it promised.

Part 4

I spent three months of my fourth year on an internship with two designers, Marius Schwarz and Zuzana Kostelanská, in Amsterdam. It was supposed to be the culmination of everything, the moment when education meets profession.

What I discovered was an enormous distance between what I’d been taught and what design practice actually required. Not a distance in technical skills, luckily I could use the software, I understood and loved playing with typography and composition. The distance was deeper: design wasn’t what I’d been trained to think it was. It wasn’t primarily about expressing oneself. It was about negotiation, talking to clients who didn’t care for design language, defending ideas without being precious about them, accepting criticism that had nothing to do with the design itself and everything to do with budgets and timelines and what the client would understand.

In What Design Can’t Do, Silvio Lorusso examines the fundamental gap between school logic and work logic. Schools are designed around learning, around experimentation and failure as necessary parts of the process. But design practice operates on entirely different logic. It’s about labor and results. Lorusso shows that schools can’t prepare you for this transition because they’re not designed to. They operate in different worlds.

But why does this gap exist? Why doesn’t education prepare students for the work they’ll most likely end up doing?

Maybe the gap serves a purpose. kabk tries to harvest both artists and designers, promising openness and fluidity to accommodate any artistic practice post-graduation. But by trying to accommodate everyone, the school fails to prepare anyone for anything specific. Each teacher has their own pedagogical vision, but with four or five of them applying that vision to you simultaneously, it becomes incoherent. The institution maintains the fiction that design is about creativity and vision because acknowledging it’s mostly about labor and compromise would undermine this promise of openness. The gap protects the institution’s prestige while ensuring graduates enter the workforce unprepared, still believing in the mythology. And you can’t win here. Try to do everything and you fail at everything.

Marius and Zuzana were generous. I learned an enormous amount. But I kept returning to the same question: why hadn’t anyone prepared me for this? Why was the distance between what I’d learned in school and what I needed to know in practice so vast and so obvious?

This was when I recognized the complete erasure. That idealized vision I'd carried into first year, the one where real learning happens, where progressive pedagogy creates engaged, critical, capable practitioners, it didn't just crack or fade. It collapsed entirely. What fell away was any remaining trust that the institution knew what it was preparing me and my peers for, that having the degree would translate into preparedness, that there was predictability.

Most days during the internship, Marius and I would sit down for lunch. We'd set up the table, make coffee, make sandwiches out of whatever was in the fridge, and immediately fall into conversation. One afternoon he said that even though he graduated from Rietveld twelve years ago, he still doesn't know how to be a “valid” designer. He believes no one really knows anything.

I found it both funny and sad. Here was someone whose work I admired, who clearly knew what he was doing, admitting that the promise of mastery was false. The idea that school would teach you enough, that eventually you'd know what you're doing – it doesn't happen. Not after four years, not after twelve.

I mentioned to him that maybe this was something I could write about. The gap wasn't just my problem. It wasn't that kabk had uniquely failed me. Even working designers, years into their practice, feel similarly. The distance between what education promises and what actually happens doesn't close with time or experience. It's structural.

The essay research that followed became a way to work through this. I wasn't researching a neat design topic with clear boundaries. I was researching education itself: what I'd experienced, the distance between what education promises and what it delivers, what alternatives might exist.

The questions I was asking didn’t have answers within the system that generated them. I could identify the problems: the exploitation of teaching labor, the gap between educational promise and delivery, the impossible binds that trap both teachers and students, the way institutions absorb and neutralize alternatives. I could even build alternatives, create the learning spaces I wanted to see. Sasha and I had done that with Playdate. I was doing it through peer teaching and tutoring.

But the fundamental structural problems remained. Building alternatives doesn’t solve them. It reveals them more clearly, but it doesn’t change the conditions that make alternatives necessary in the first place.

What does it mean that we have to build alternatives to institutions that claim to provide what we need? What does it mean that institutions promise one thing and systematically deliver another? What happens when the alternatives we build get absorbed back into institutional logic, when Playdate becomes a line on a CV or a model for “student initiative” that the institution can point to during the Open Day without actually supporting?

Can teaching exist outside these binds? Can you commit fully to teaching without becoming professionally invisible? Can you maintain your own practice without treating teaching as exploited labor? Can institutions support learning that emerges from collective desire rather than curriculum planning?

I don’t have answers to these questions. I have four years of experience that taught me to ask them. I have alternatives I’ve built that work within their limitations. I have a commitment to teaching that exists alongside the knowledge that teaching within institutions means accepting impossible conditions.

I’m graduating with a degree from a prestigious institution. I understand more clearly than ever how little that means, how vast the distance is between what school promises and tries to predict for me and what is to come. I’m committed to teaching, to building alternatives, to creating learning spaces outside institutional control.

But I’m not pretending that commitment solves the structural problems I’ve identified. The bind remains. The exploitation continues. The gap between promise and reality persists.

I’m in the wreckage of my idealized vision of education, surrounded by the ruins of institutional trust. I’m building something, but I don’t know if what I’m building can escape the conditions that made it necessary. I’m still questioning, still figuring out what I actually believe.

I’m no longer waiting for answers from above. But I’m also not pretending the answers I’m constructing are sufficient. The problems are structural. The alternatives are necessary. Neither of these facts resolves the other.

I don't know!
